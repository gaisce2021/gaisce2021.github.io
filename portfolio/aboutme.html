About Me

A snapshot of the creator

Welcome to my Gaisce Portfolio

Hey there, I'm Orla. I am a young woman in my 20s completing what has been a long but enjoyable adventure; three Gaisce awards in succession. I began my Bronze journey during the second year of my undergraduate degree. I continued with the Silver Award in the third year of my degree. I completed those activities in time to Go for Gold over the summer shortly before entering my final year.

Signing Up

My journey started in April 2021. We were in the midst of what felt like the millionth lockdown when I decided to undertake my first Gaisce journey. The coverage was wall to wall on every news channel, dominating every newspaper in the shops; Prince Philip had passed away at the ripe old age of 99. Much of the coverage focused on the Duke of Edinburgh Award and I couldn't help but thinking to myself that this sounds just like Gaisce from when I was in school.

I was a shy child and a shy teenager. My school offered students the opportunity to complete a Gaisce Award in transition year. About 2-3 dozen students finished the year with a smattering of bronze and silver pieces of pocket sized metal awards and another certificate for their folder of achievement which we had to compile and present to keep us occupied throughout fourth year (my point of pride that year was the Microsoft certificates from the one of the few classes that year I really enjoyed). I had considered Gaisce that year, but I was too shy and too self-conscious. There'll be some terrible picture of you on the school website when they present an award to you in May. You'll be the slowest person on the hike, you definitely won't last a full 25km with your legs. What if you join a local sports club and someone who bullied you trains there too? You'll definitely end up sharing a bedroom with one of the most annoying people from the year just like you got landed with that person last year? So while the interest was there, the confidence was most certainly absent in my teenage self. Even today I am still somewhat private; only using my first name on this website and having a rule against posting photos of myself (particularly anything identifying like my face) online. I saw the opportunity and I left it pass me by.

Let's skip forward to April 2021 when we had thirteen months of on again, off again lockdowns. While I felt alright during the initial March 2020 lockdowns and enjoyed a break from busy city living as a college student, by this point I wasn't doing so good. I was back in my rural home; no car, no internship with the uncertain market, and above all, no purpose. Once the news about Prince Philip broke, I began to reconsider the opportunity in front of me. Look just do the bronze and if you don't like it, just slap it on your CV and call it a day. I hummed and hawed for a few days before checking the Gaisce website, sending an email and taking the plunge.

Bronze

I felt a little bit alien after sending that initial email but was soon matched up with a PAL who also happened to be called Orla. Covid lockdowns were still in place, so I couldn't leave my house to do activities like my classmates had a number of years prior. I decided to go about setting up an Instagram account to document my progress in case I couldn't find anyone to sign off on my sheets. My community involvement was cooking. This took the pressure off my parents, both of whom were working in person, and allowed me to learn a new skill. My physical recreation was yoga. I had already begun yoga during a previous lockdown which made it a natural continuation. I chose to undertake an extra 13 weeks of yoga as this was the activity I was already most familiar with. My personal skill was book reviews. I loved reading as a child but stopped as a teenager due to the volume of paper I would look at each day at home and in classes. Reviewing books allowed me to get back into reading and to really digest books at an adult level. My adventure journey took place in Tipperary. Up until 2021, this was a county that I had only ever seen on the Cork-Dublin motorway and from the forecourt of a petrol station the one time we needed to stop for diesel.

Silver

Life had quasi returned to normal by time I chose to continue and move from Bronze to Silver. Lectures were both live and recorded, everything was open but masks were still compulsory. I decided to change my activities entirely. My community involvement was teaching guitar. I had been playing guitar since I was a primary school student which gave me enough experience and knowledge to pass that information on to others. It also gave me the chance to begin this website and dabble in web development which I've always had an interest in. My physical recreation was taekwondo. I signed up to one of the clubs in college and attended training for an hour and a half either once or twice a week depending on the number of assignments I had due. My personal skill was playing guitar. This linked up nicely with my community involvement. I hadn't played much since my Leaving Cert Music exam so this gave me a chance to rediscover an old passion of mine. I capped off the experience with a hike in Kerry. My adventure journey was a 3-day hike from Tralee to Dingle. In typical Irish fashion, I experienced a different type of weather each day.

Gold

I signed up for my Gold Award the same week I had finished my Silver Award. I had begun my activities before my Bronze Award had a friend to join it on my desk. This has been the toughest leg of the journey due to change and grief in my personal life and I took longer to complete my Gold Award than I had hoped.

Once again I changed activities to keep things interesting. My community involvement was teaching Python programming. This allowed me to review my notes throughout college and create something which I could talk about in interviews while also sharing my knowledge of a topic I am passionate about. My physical recreation was gym-work. I intentionally didn't pick a specific sport for fear that my final year workload would interfere with my ability to attend training and I would lose access to the college gym once my student card expired. I was glad I picked gym-work as it gave me a chance to try different forms of exercise based on what my body needed that week. My personal skill was artwork. Art was one of my Junior Cert subjects which dampened my joy for a number of years. Returning to art as an adult allowed me to use a medium I prefer and allowing me to take a step back from using screens.

Reflections

Creating this portfolio has been both an interesting and a worthwhile task. I wish I had a notebook to record my activities in more depth. I'm definitely sorry that I couldn't provide more detail on all of my activities and trips. I would definitely advise someone starting out today to scribble down little notes as they complete their journey. I'm glad I created an Instagram account to photograph and document my time working towards each of the three awards. As covid dissipated and there was less of a need to document my award progress, I slipped back on posting which is something I regret. I have slowly been searching for my old photos so that I feel my record has been truly completed.

The upside of the last few years is having the chance to try new things while also remaining true to my roots and checking back in with the hobbies I enjoyed growing up. The older we get, the less open we can be towards stepping out of our comfort zone. I'm glad that I had enough confidence to take that initial step back in 2021 and have the willingness to do something a little different. I've also had the chance to add my volunteer work to my CV and learn some new skills, both hard skills and soft skills, which will benefit me in the world of work.

Acknowledgements

Before I close I'd like to acknowledge the team at Gaisce, in particular the hard work of my fantastic PAL Orla. I'd like to thank a handful of my lecturers throughout both my undergraduate and postgraduate degrees for showing patience towards the class, for making abstract concepts simple, for providing interesting assignments to allow me to apply theoretical knowledge to real world problems. I would also like to thank some of the mentors (such as my taekwondo trainer in my college club) for teaching me new skills and getting me to the point where I could pass the baton on and teach others new things. Thank you to my friends who kept me going throughout the last few years, through both good days and bad days, and for giving me help, advice and a chance to let my hair down when I needed it.
