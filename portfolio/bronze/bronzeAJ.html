Tipperary

Hiking in the Premier County

Selecting a Venue

My adventure journey is one of the things, as I write this in 2024, that I wish I had documented in greater detail. I visited Tipperary in one of the gaps in one of our many lockdowns. It was pretty awkward planning anything back in 2020 and 2021 for fear that the country would've shut down again at short notice. My birthday had fallen during a lockdown so my mother and I were planning on going away once the weather (and the covid situation) were less of an issue for a belated celebration. I ended up combining the trip and my adventure journey for fear that the country would shut down again.

I had never been to Tipperary at that time. My mother had been there before with her hillwalking group which she was an active member of when I was a child. I had only seen the country through the window of a car while my parents drove between Cork and Dublin on the motorway. I wasn't really sure what was there but my mother told me that Tipperary had some good scenery and nice walks, so I was happy to try a new county. Here's a map of each county I've been to as of August 2024.

We went to Tipperary in September 2021. We did a mix of walking and driving while we stayed away. We saw a mix of sights in Cashel, Cahir and the Glen of Aherlow. I had previously cycled the Waterford Greenway, so I was aware that Tipperary had mountains. This was my first time getting to see some views from that height. I was surprised by how scenic Tipperary was.

I haven't been back to Tipperary since. I would like to get a chance to return to see some of the museums which were closed at the time due to covid. The Swiss Cottage is one particular place which we were unable to enter due to restrictions at the time. Perhaps I would also visit the northern half of Tipperary as my adventure journey predominantly focused on the southern half of the county.

Where to Walk

The tourist office is usually a good place to go looking for information about the local area. My mother and I stopped and picked up a number of brochures including a little folder of hikes in the area. The Galtee Mountains are located in the area in addition to the Suir Blueway. You can also complete the Lough Derg Way which begins in Limerick and ends in Tipperary. Here's a list of some longer walking trails, some of which are suitable for adventure journeys.

  • St Declan's Way - 116.6 km - Cashel to Ardmore, Waterford
  • Ballyhoura Way - 89km - Near Kanturk, Cork to Limerick Junction, Tipperary
  • East Munster Way - 69km - Carrick-on-Suir to Clogheen
  • The Lough Derg Way - 68km - Limerick City to Dromineer, Tipperary
  • Tipperary Heritage Way - 59.6 km - Clogheen to Cashel
  • The Suir Blueway - 53km - Carrick-on-Suir to Cahir
    • By land - 21km - Carrick-on-Suir to Clonmel
    • By water - 32km - Clonmel to Cahir
  • The Slieve Felim Way - 43km - Toor to Silvermines

Perhaps you're living in Tipperary, and you'd like to practice hiking in advance of completing your adventure journey. Some of these walks may be suitable for someone passing through the area for a day trip too.

  • Sli Eala - 10.4km - Dromineer to Nenagh Bridge
  • Ballinacourty Loop - 10km - Glen of Aherlow
  • Eamonn an Chnoic Loop - 8km - Upperchurch
  • Bay Lough Loop - 4.5km - Killballyboy Woods
  • The Beech Trail - 2.5km - Knocknacree Woods

The trails above were compiled with the information provided by Sport Ireland and Tipperary Tourism. I only needed to complete 25km over the course of the two days I spent in the county. I walked along the Suir Blueway, starting a bit back from the starting point of Cahir Castle by walking around the town first. I also strolled through the woods in the Glen of Aherlow. While it was more than I would walk on an average day, it was still within reason of the average young person's ability to walk.

What to See

Cahir Castle is located at the start of the Suir Blueway. We visited the castle which, like all OPW heritage sites, was free to see at the time. The castle dates back to the 13th century and was owned by the Butlers, an Anglo-Norman family. The castle has been involved in a number of battles. It was captured three times; in 1599, in 1647, and finally 1650. It passed to the Irish State in 1961 when the last Lord Cahir passed away.

The inside of the castle was open to visitors albeit with masks and garish yellow covid signage. There was an exhibit detailing the role of women in medieval Ireland in both English and Irish. There were also some old pieces of furniture on display such as chairs and ornate storage cabinets. The outside had a neatly cut grassy courtyard and a map of the castle grounds built into the cobbled floor outside.

The Swiss Cottage is located a little further along the walkway. We walked around the outside of the building. We did not see the inside as it was closed due to covid restrictions in place at the time. The outside is very picturesque with its thatched roof. The cottage looks like something from a fairytale or like a house you could find illustrated in a children's book like Heidi. It is really distinct in comparison other styles of architecture I have seen within Ireland. The cottage is white on the outside with black detailing like old/traditional German housing. The Swiss Cottage was built in the early 1800s by Richard Butler, the 1st Earl of Glengall. It is currently in OPW hands.

The Rock of Cashel is arguably Tipperary's most famous landmark. We took the car to Cashel rather than visiting on foot. We were able to see outer grounds of the castle but were unable to see the inside at the time. This was the busiest of the three sites we visited over the two days. It juts out above the landscape and allows visitors see for miles around. The Rock of Cashel was the seat of the Kings of Munster prior to the Norman invasion. The site itself boasts a 12th century chapel and round tower (which were closed due to covid), a much larger 13th century cathedral which had no roof that I was able to see, as well as a 15th century residential tower. There is also a graveyard located on the hill which is still in use. The most recent burial here took place in 2023.
