Guitar Teacher

26 Lessons. 6 Strings.

Selecting a Skill

Covid restrictions had somewhat dissipated by October 2021 when I began working towards my Silver Award. However, I still went home to my rural house at weekends from college and I didn't have a car. Local nursing homes were, understandably, probably somewhat hesitant to allow visitors meet the residents. It left me questioning what I could do to complete the 26 weeks of community involvement.

My PAL Orla and I tried to come up with some ideas together. We eventually settled on me teaching guitar lessons. I was already playing guitar as my personal skill (which you can read more about here). She paired me with another college student who planned to learn to play the guitar as her personal skill. Unfortunately this fell through and I didn't start my community involvement until January.

Orla and I spoke again as I was progressing with my physical recreation and personal skill but was unable to begin my community involvement. She came up with the fantastic suggestion of creating a website and uploading lessons there. I had previously completed an internship which began my journey in web development. I used the skills I had learned there to create a website to host my guitar lessons.

Creating Lessons

The first step in the journey was finding a place to host my website. I was an unemployed college student so my preferred cost was free. I began some research into hosting options. I found out that GitHub allows people create their own website and host it for free. I set up a second GitHub account separate to the one under my own name where I host my college projects. I followed a tutorial to get my website up and running before moving onto the next task.

My second job before uploading content was to create the styling of the page. I created a little sample About Me page before uploading any content to the website. This had a dual purpose. Firstly I wanted to describe my musical background to any potential learners who stumbled across my page. The second reason was to have a test page to see how my webpages would render. The skeleton of the website was created using HTML and the colour and fonts were injected into the website with CSS. I did use a small amount of JavaScript to make some of my lessons interactive by adding buttons and a quiz.

Once the bulk of the tech side was over and done with, I mainly focused on creating content. My personal skill was playing the guitar. As it was already underway, I had already dug up all of my music books and my old folders of sheet music. This helped me remember back to my initial guitar lessons in primary school and put me into the shoes of a beginner. I used my first guitar book to find the chords that I first learned and which were the most beginner-friendly. There was a certain amount of work that took place as well as content creation as I needed to find appropriate songs for a learner to play between lesson uploads.

In addition to my guitar playing skills, I also had a more theoretical background than the average guitarist. I decided to include a certain amount of theory that would not have been included in my own lessons but which I feel made me a better musician. The earlier lessons focused on learning basic chords so that any learner could begin to feel comfortable with common chords before learning more difficult chords like barre chords and awkwardly shaped chords which are more advanced.

I decided to theme some of the later lessons around a particular genre of music to keep things interesting. One week was focused around country music but there were also entire lessons devoted to music from musicals and genres like pop and jazz. I tried to include songs from different years that both older and younger learners would be familiar with so as not to alienate anyone viewing the website. I also spent a certain amount of time transcribing music to put in a separate page on that website so that chords would render above the correct lyrics. I created some small snippets of music for students to play simple melodies using software called MuseScore.

I ensured that students learned a mix of styles of playing in addition to genres, so they would be more complete musicians. I included chords to allow students learn to play popular songs. I included sheet music and tab to allow students play the melody of a song. I included scales to allow students build up their finger strength and get used to the spacing between frets when moving from one note to the next. I also added some guitar techniques such as slides, bends and vibrato in later lessons which are slightly more advanced but make songs sound much more interesting. I wanted to give beginners a mix of theoretical and practical lessons; a mix of melody and harmony; a mix of lead and rhythm guitar; a mix of playing someone else's song and composing your own.

Teaching Guitar Today

I finished working on my website in summer 2022. I really liked the chance to work on a project that drew on multiple parts of my background. This made my website into something which I don't think everyone could've created. Teaching guitar involved a mix of musical ability, technical skills to create and maintain a website, knowledge of the guitar, and the ability to pass that on to the next person. While the website is no longer being updated, the 26 lessons do remain online and can be found here if you would like to play music.

I drew on the communication skills I had built up during my time creating book reviews during my time working towards my Bronze Award. I've worn many hats in my life but up to that point teacher would not have been one of them. I think that to be a good teacher you need to be a good communicator. How can a student understand what you're trying to teach them if you're not articulate enough to pass on that knowledge? A good teacher also needs to put themselves in a learner's shoes. One of my favourite lecturers from my undergraduate degree began his first lesson with us by stating I was in your position 13 years ago. His lessons were some of the best attended classes throughout the four years as he was patient with a first year class that had a range of scholarship recipients and people who repeated the module that year. Having patience, the ability to speak plainly to transfer knowledge, and having some empathy for a learner goes a long way in determining whether someone is a good teacher or not.

Unlike most guitarists I know, I can read sheet music. This has helped me pick up piano books in my house and play both the melody and the harmony of a song. Reading sheet music allows you to play a wider range of music. I also provided scales and arpeggios, commonly learned by piano players, so that students would get used to hearing which notes belonged together in a particular key. I provided chord progressions which are used together often in songs for any students interested in composing their own songs. This drew on my time in secondary school when I studied Music as a Leaving Certificate subject and received a high honours grade in addition to receiving distinction grades in Music Theory exams.

I had previously spent time doing an internship and getting to grips with web development prior to commencing teaching guitar online. I had some technical knowledge which was expanded via my community involvement within the Gaisce Award program. Creating a website of my own made me realise how important it is to make the website accessible to the disabled community. I ensured that text and background had a sufficient colour contrast so that students could easily read what was on their screen. I added alternative text to photos so that a blind or visually impaired person using a screen reader could "see" the picture on my webpage. This was useful for the next bit of community involvement I later embarked on, you can find out a little bit more about that here.
