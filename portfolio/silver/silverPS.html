Strumming Through Styles

Selecting a Skill

I first began to play the guitar when I was a student in primary school. It was the first year that we had the option to start guitar lessons in the evenings after school. I begged my parents to buy me a guitar as no members of my household were guitarists themselves. My parents relented, likely because I was already attending piano lessons at the time, and bought me one. I used to walk to my school one evening each week that year with my guitar and the book our teacher asked us to buy. I remember how difficult I found playing a B7 chord at the time.

I played guitar more regularly in my early teens and got relatively good relatively quickly. I hit a bit of a ceiling in my abilities and dialed back the amount of time I spent playing the guitar in my mid-teens. I returned to playing more regularly and challenging myself from the end of 4th year onwards. While I wasn't a Junior Cert Music student, I did choose the subject for my Leaving Certificate. Half of the exam for the subject was practical which lead to me playing guitar for the purposes of my education for the first time in my life.

I spent a good part of 6th year playing the same four songs on repeat in the lead up to my practical exam in April. Once the exam was out of the way, I discarded playing the guitar for a number of years. I think I was a little turned off by having what was a hobby become something I needed to perfect for months on end.

While I found guitar playing the perfect hobby during secondary school, it took a back seat to reading during college. I had been out of practice for a little while so when it came to choosing a personal skill I decided to give my formerly beloved hobby another chance. It linked in nicely with my community involvement and allowed me to review the level of difficulty of a song for my lessons.

Getting Started

I went through the collection of books and folders I had stored up in my house to kick things off. I remembered buying a few books a few years prior to learn to play new styles in addition to what I could already play. I still have my original book from 10-15 years ago which I learned to play basic chords like A, E and D from. I also had folders with printed sheets of songs and chords which I had collected from my guitar teachers down through the years.

I began with the easier songs in my collection of sheet music. Like riding a bike or driving a car, you never truly forget how to play the guitar. Even if I read F and then G on a page, my left hand knew exactly where each finger needs to be so that my right hand didn't need to skip a beat while strumming. After playing any instrument for a certain amount of time, I think you will unconsciously build up that muscle memory. While I definitely wasn't at the heights I had reached when I was in my early teens, I was more than comfortable playing through each song in my original guitar book; The Complete Guitar Player by Russ Shipton.

I also had an older Taylor Swift book which was published around the time she was releasing her earlier albums like Speak Now and Red which definitely translate over to guitar better than some of her newer material. I had some books that were a little bit more technical in nature. Guitar is a bit of an outlier as most guitarists I know cannot read sheet music unless they play a second instrument. I picked up some books like Time Pieces for Guitar as well as my existing collection of the Little Black Songbook range of books which focused on particular artists like Oasis. I also would try to play songs from the piano books we had in the house as I could read from the treble and bass clefs.

I'd definitely tell anyone who lasts a few months playing a guitar and wants to play more seriously to invest in a capo. I didn't have one for about 2 years myself; it makes a massive difference to your ability to play more songs unless you can transpose music. I'd also tell someone learning not to neglect their ears. I can tune my guitar by ear as well as work out how to play the melody of some of the songs I've listened to a lot. You can try finding the sheet music online and hide it while you're training your ears to pick up which notes are being played in the song. Even looking at the starting note and trying to figure it out can be really helpful.

Guitar Today

I think my relationship with guitar playing is similar to how it was before I started my Silver Award back in October 2021. I find today that I go through "on periods" where I'll pick up my guitar and play through entire books and albums in my house. I will also go through "off periods" where I'm not in the mood to play and my guitar will sit there for months at a time gathering dust. It probably doesn't help that the guitar can be a louder instrument than the piano. I definitely find I'm more likely to play the guitar today when my family are out of the house which is definitely hard to do when one of my parents has a mostly remote working arrangement.

I definitely feel that I was a more enthusiastic guitarist when I was about 13-14. I had received an electric guitar and an amplifier as a Christmas gift around that time and I listened to music far more often then compared to now. I still listen to music but definitely not to the extent I would've on the bus rides home from school. I usually spend my time walking listening to voice notes from friends or the occasional podcast sent my way. Perhaps reconnecting with the old bands that I used to listen to nonstop as well as finding modern artists who produce more guitar friendly songs than what's typically on the radio could spark my old passion for playing the guitar.
