Taekwondo

예의, 염치, 인내, 극기, 백절불굴

Selecting a Skill

I spent 26 weeks doing yoga in order to receive my Bronze Award. I decided to try a new sport for the following 26 weeks as a Silver Award participant. I was changing all my other activities anyway both to keep things interesting for me and as a little bit of a challenge. I was living in student accommodation at the time and the lack of space made yoga an unsuitable choice. I scrolled through the available clubs in college and I found taekwondo listed as one amongst the list.

I had previously done some taekwondo when I was in primary school. I really liked it, but I stopped by time I entered secondary school. My club was based in a nearby community hall in a local village. Due to numbers of kids attending, it wasn't feasible for the instructors to continue holding classes there, so they merged our group with another class in a town about twice as far from my home. My friends from primary school were dropping out one by one around that time until it was just me. I did attend the club for some time after the merger, but unfortunately I left a little while after that. I was roughly 10 years old at the time. The other members of the club were a mix of 5-7 year old boys and teenagers. I didn't know any other students and there was no one in my age group who I could get to know. With the sole exception of my taekwondo instructors giving us a workshop in 4th year, I didn't practice the sport for about a decade.

Getting Started

While there were still some restrictions in place, the gym was up and running again in college. Club training had returned. I knew that starting the sport in October meant that I would be able to complete the 26 weeks while I was in college. I remember needing to log into the clubs portal and book a slot for training at the time for the purposes of contact tracing and to keep numbers low. Eventually restrictions were pared back and entirely disappeared over time.

Clubs in college were surprisingly welcoming. I liked taekwondo anyway but even going in with a positive mindset I was surprised how welcoming people were. I was worried I would stick out like a sore thumb, that I had been too out of practice after a break of multiple years, that I wouldn't fit into some pre-existing clique like had happened throughout my life; instead everyone was pretty chill. Looking back, joining a club was one of the best things I did throughout my undergrad and I would highly recommend it to anyone else.

Training began each night with an oath. Taekwondo has five tenets courtesy, integrity, perseverance, self-control, indomitable spirit which were recited at the beginning of the class. If you scroll to the top of the page, you can see these five tenets in Korean. Taekwondo itself is a Korean martial art. It is one of the many sports shown in the Olympics every four years.

Training kicked off with warming up. I was fairly flexible after spending months doing yoga prior to attending club training, so I always liked to see how far I could stretch myself. I was able to lower myself all the way to the floor for certain stretches and stretch my hands beyond the sole of my foot for other stretches. We also worked towards doing the splits in the class. I could get pelvis roughly 2-3 inches from the ground. Our instructor, a grey haired middle-aged man, could do a full split which was something I really didn't expect as he looked like a person who would blend in with my father's group of friends.

We used to do drills walking up and down the gym floor. There was definitely a little bit of pressure to take larger steps when there was someone taller standing in the row behind you as they could catch up to you and getting a thump in the back wasn't fun. I also needed to make sure not to take steps that were too large in case I hit the mirror at the front of the room. This allowed us to focus on movements in isolation which was definitely quite important for those of us at a beginner or intermediate level. Our instructor would count in Korean from one to ten as we walked up and down the floor.

We spent a certain amount of time doing pair work while training. Sometimes this was slower drills with a partner and other times this was quicker sparring. I secretly preferred to be paired with other female students or the more advanced students in the class. The less experienced female students definitely didn't hit anywhere near as hard as the untrained male students. There were a number of black belts who also attended club training. The great thing about being paired with them, male or female, is that they knew you were a beginner; they would hold back to give you a chance to get used to the movement. They were also experienced enough to help you with your form or give you some tips. Sometimes it was fun to see two black belts sparring against each other when we rotated the group and swapped partners. They wouldn't hold back with each other and go for hell for leather with punches, kicks and blocks. One of my favourite memories is the time a guy accidentally punched me in the shoulder and apologised. It was my turn to make an aggressive move and his turn to either swerve it or block it. I kicked a little bit higher than I had intended and missed his head by an inch or two and immediately apologised. We both burst out laughing at that point. It was definitely a hazard that came with the territory.

We would usually split in two groups at one point in the class; the more experienced students and the less experienced students. We would take this time to review patterns which are a series of movements students practice when going for grading exams. The more advanced students had longer patterns to learn with tougher movements whilst ours were shorter and simpler in comparison. Sometimes our instructor would review our progress and leave the other students practice their tougher patterns by themselves. Other times we would try to perform our easier patterns in unison while he went about teaching other students more difficult patterns for their upcoming exams.

Taekwondo Today

I am sorry to say that I didn't continue to attend club training when my work placement finished and I returned to college for my final year. I had moved from student accommodation near the gym to a house which was further out in a nice suburb of the city. I ultimately didn't feel safe taking public transport at night after training. I did attend a small handful of training sessions on Saturdays through college. Between the volume of college work I had, the length of the training sessions, the amount of time it would take to travel to/from the gym, plus the personal/familial commitments I was dealing with at the time made it a non runner.

I have still tried to keep up with the movements I have done before but definitely not the extent I did with club training when I was attending 90-minute classes 1-2 times a week in the evenings. I prefer going through any patterns in my garden as our hedge gives me privacy from cars passing by our house on the road in front of the driveway. It also helps to mark my starting point on the grass as finishing a pattern in the same place that you started in is an indicator that you've done the movements correctly.

I definitely miss the sportsmanship and camaraderie or the time I spent doing club training. I met a few people through the club who I'm still connected to on social media. I would consider joining a taekwondo club in my locality. I know that my childhood instructors are still teaching in the area and now that I have a car joining a sports club is more accessible to me and less hassle for my parents.
